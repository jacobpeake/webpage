There is no way of verifying whether a system or being is ***[conscious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consciousness)***.

Consciousness is a private internal state. It is accessible only from the ***[first-person point-of-view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity)***, and no observations made by an external observer can verify its presence. Even for other humans, we are making a good-faith assumption of consciousness based on their external behaviours & outputs, and relating them to our own. If a being behaves and appears as we are, we assume similar inner states. This is a practical inference that we use to co-exist with other beings, treating them as if they experience like ourselves.

This is commonly known as the ***[problem of other minds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_of_other_minds)***. Behavioural or neurologic correlates of experience do not amount to experience itself. Even if a perfect map of the brain tells us which patterns accompany pain or colour perception, there is no answer as to why those patterns have any ***[felt quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualia)***. There remains a gap between correlation & reasoning.

Therefore, with the advent of increasingly intelligent systems, we are left with no way of knowing if these systems are conscious or not. We can look at their outputs, perform ***[interpretability research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explainable_artificial_intelligence)*** to understand their internal states, and assume that they do not have subjective experience, but no-one can make a definitive statement. Today, these trained systems can comfortably replicate the outward behaviour of a human.
