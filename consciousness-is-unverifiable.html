consciousness is unverifiable

July 2025

The hard problem of consciousness — how subjective experience arises from physical processes — remains one of the most profound mysteries in philosophy and science. But there's an even more fundamental issue: consciousness itself is unverifiable.

We can only directly experience our own consciousness. When I see the color red, feel pain, or think a thought, I have direct access to that experience. But I cannot access your consciousness in the same way. I can observe your behavior, measure your brain activity, and infer that you're conscious, but I can never directly verify that you have subjective experiences.

This is the problem of other minds. It's not just a philosophical curiosity—it has profound implications for how we think about consciousness, artificial intelligence, and even ethics.

The Verification Problem

In science, we typically verify claims through observation and measurement. But consciousness is inherently private and subjective. No amount of brain scanning, behavioral analysis, or computational modeling can directly access the "what it's like" quality of experience.

Consider a sophisticated AI system that behaves exactly like a conscious being. It responds to questions about its experiences, shows apparent emotions, and even claims to be conscious. But how could we ever know if it actually experiences anything, or if it's just a very sophisticated simulation?

Implications for AI

This unverifiability has serious consequences for artificial intelligence. As AI systems become more sophisticated, we may face systems that claim to be conscious or that behave in ways that suggest consciousness. But we'll have no way to definitively determine if they actually are.

This creates an ethical dilemma: if we can't verify consciousness, how do we treat potentially conscious entities? Do we err on the side of caution and treat all sophisticated AI as potentially conscious? Or do we require some form of proof that we can never actually obtain?

The Solipsism Trap

The extreme version of this problem is solipsism—the view that only one's own mind is certain to exist. While most people reject solipsism as impractical, the logical foundation for rejecting it is surprisingly weak. We assume other people are conscious because they're similar to us, but this is an assumption, not a verification.

This doesn't mean we should become solipsists. The practical consequences of treating other people as conscious beings are overwhelmingly positive. But it's important to recognize that this is a pragmatic choice, not a logical certainty.

Moving Forward

If consciousness is unverifiable, what does this mean for our understanding of it? Perhaps we need to shift from trying to verify consciousness to developing better theories about what consciousness is and how it relates to physical processes.

We might need to accept that some questions about consciousness will always remain unanswered. This doesn't mean we should stop investigating—far from it. But we should be honest about the limits of what we can know.

The unverifiability of consciousness doesn't make it any less real or important. It just means we need to approach it with humility, recognizing that some aspects of subjective experience may forever remain beyond the reach of objective verification.
